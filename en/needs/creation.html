Cognitive & creative

◇Creation

Inventing, expressing, producing

Creating, inventing, producing. Creating is worth doing for its own sake, not for the outcome. It opens a path when performance no longer suffices — a way to inhabit the world without needing to win anything.

✦

Essence
  • Creating is a personal way of inhabiting the world — leaving a mark, without claiming to speak for all.
  • The state of happy concentration while creating is satisfying in itself — regardless of the result.[1]
  • Writing or making something artistic eases what cannot be said in any other way.[2][3]
  • Creating is worth practising even without recognised talent — the value lies in the act, not in how it will be received (e.g. keeping a notebook no one will see, or improvising on the piano without an audience).
⚠

Signs of absence
  • A sense of inner emptiness, deep boredom even in a life that is objectively full (e.g. a smooth and outwardly successful life, with the vague sense that "it isn't really me living it").
  • Difficulty putting into words or expressing what we feel inside.
  • Consuming culture passively without ever producing anything oneself.
  • Perfectionism that blocks everything before we have even started.
→

Ways in
  • Practise regularly without judging yourself: keep a journal, sketch, improvise, cook, take photos.
  • Separate creation from outside evaluation — create first for yourself, only then for an audience.
  • Set aside each week a protected, non-negotiable time to create.
  • Accept making "bad work" — it is a necessary stage, and where the good begins.
✕

Pitfalls
Pseudo-satisfier — what does Max-Neef mean?

Max-Neef names a pseudo-satisfier anything that takes the place of a response to the need without filling the role. The pitfalls below are the forms specific to creation.

  • Creating while chasing external metrics — likes, sales, algorithms (e.g. only posting a photo if it is likely to "do well", until you forget why you took it).
  • Waiting passively for inspiration — it tends to arrive in the act, rarely before.
  • Comparing oneself to professionals when what we are looking for is not the same as theirs.
  • Over-constraining oneself with performance goals that kill the pleasure of making.
