◈Affection

Loving and letting oneself be loved

Loving and feeling loved is no luxury. The quality of our bonds counts as much as food or movement for our health and the taste of life. It plays out in a few deep relationships, not in a large number of contacts.

✦

Essence
  • A deep bond gives meaning — it connects us to someone who knows who we are, to whom we speak, and to whom we are missed when absent.[1]
  • The quality of our relationships changes how stress moves through us.[2]
  • Receiving love is practised, just as much as giving it — often it is even the harder of the two.
  • The quality and presence of social ties matter for health as much as factors like smoking or physical activity — this is what studies following over 300,000 people have shown.[3]
⚠

Signs of absence
  • Feeling lonely even though one sees many people.[4]
  • Difficulty welcoming the affection received — as if it were not meant for us.
  • Endlessly seeking validation, without really investing in return.
  • Always feeling worn out by relationships, or avoiding them in advance out of fear.
→

Ways in
  • Understand one's own attachment style — more secure, anxious, or avoidant — to better recognise one's reactions in relationships.[5]
  • Invest in a few deep relationships (three to five), rather than scattering time over many contacts.
  • Dare to show a fragility — but only with someone who can receive it without judging.
  • Reduce the invisible barriers: overstuffed calendars, screens everywhere, attention constantly interrupted.
✕

Pitfalls
  • Piling up online contacts without ever building a deep bond.
  • Relying on a single person and letting friends and family drop away.
  • Stringing together relationships without ever committing — intimacy stays forever postponed.
  • Replacing concrete bonds with attachment to a cause, an abstract group, or an admired figure seen from afar.
