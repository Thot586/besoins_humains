Continuity and coherence of self

A continuity with oneself, a stable value, a coherence between what we think and what we do. After a big goal, an identity built only on performance wavers: we then have to find what remains once the role fades. That is often where life really begins again.

✦

Essence
  • Coherence between one's values and one's actions feeds meaning day after day.[1]
  • Telling one's own life story (past, present, future) builds identity — it is told, reshaped, refined over time.[2]
  • Being kind to oneself (self-compassion) helps hold steady through mood swings and failures.[3]
  • Learning to recognise oneself without depending on others' approval — a lifelong task.
⚠

Signs of absence
  • A sense of identity emptiness, instability of self — especially after a loss or a big achievement (e.g. landing the dream job and no longer knowing who you are without that project, or losing a loved one and feeling a part of yourself disappear with them).
  • Adapting to each person like a chameleon, until one no longer knows who one is.
  • Being very sensitive to even small criticism, dwelling on it long after.[4]
  • Instability in major life choices; decisions questioned endlessly.
→

Ways in
  • Distinguish one's true values (those that really belong to us) from values we adopted without choosing (from family, society) (e.g. asking whether "being successful" still matters once you mute the family voice that keeps repeating it). Clarifying values is a well-studied lever.[5][6]
  • Act in line with one's values without waiting for outside approval — even when no one is watching.
  • Practise self-compassion regularly — speak to yourself as you would speak to a dear friend.
  • Build an integrated life story in which every chapter has its place, including the failures.
✕

Pitfalls

Pseudo-satisfier — what does Max-Neef mean?

Max-Neef names a pseudo-satisfier anything that takes the place of a response to the need without filling the role. The pitfalls below are the forms specific to identity.

  • Identity that depends entirely on others' recognition — success, applause, the gaze.
  • Showy purchases as a signal of who we are — we end up defining ourselves by what we own (e.g. piling up visible labels so others can "guess" who we are).
  • Comparing oneself downward to feel better at someone else's expense.
  • Clinging to a rigid ideology to avoid doubt — certainty replaces reflection.
